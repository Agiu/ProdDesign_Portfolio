## The Challenge

Popular storefronts, whether gaming or everyday products, tend to be messy and too transactional. Yes, storefronts are meant for transaction, but where’s the fun in letting the products speak for themselves. As an example, Netflix pushes for ultimate catalogue immersion when sifting through movies — letting the user watch a brief preview while letting the movie take over half of the screen utilized for that brief persuasion period. Then it lets the user continue sifting if they so choose.

### Currently the Steam Storefront Looks as Such

Steam / Valve Software have made strides to fix up the UX as soon as the player clicks on the store section of the steam application. However, once you start digging deeper, there’s so many distractions that could lead the player in not finding exactly what they need.

### Everchanging Platform As a Driving Force

Everything is organic and mutable on Steam, the algorithms that feed you certain games are based on your own playing patterns; in other words it's very personalized and social—it has many social proofs.

So why edit something that already works better than intended? ==I’d rather reframe the shopping experience than completely redesign the layouts of the steam platforms. I personally think that the e-shopping experience can be improved. Even the quotes from above hint at it. ==

It turned into a metaphor about the current state of gaming, screens are the most important part of the game selection process, not game boxes.

## Building Up Context Behind Steam | ==Market (Competitive) and IA Teardown analysis==


Valve's identity is industrial and open. The aesthetic is clean and analytical, pulled straight from Portal, which they still use as a tech demo, and the brand language is mechanical down to the names: Steam, Valve, Steamworks, SteamPipe. Even the logo, a piston rod and crank, puts the machine's inner workings on display, the opposite of what most products do. That exposure is deliberate: Valve is rooted in the modding community, ships open-source SDKs, and is built to let people work on top of its platform rather than around it.

That same instinct shaped Steam. It began as software-delivery infrastructure, which was a way to patch Valve's own games on the fly ==(Joelsson, Hyrynsalmi & Molenaar, 2018)==, and the storefront grew on top of it. By 2008, Steamworks let developers publish directly, turning a store into a prosumer ecosystem: people build, Valve maintains, and everyone keeps it alive together for the most part. Steam was never designed as a shop first.

==So the philosophy is simple: Valve builds in the open and puts the user first. The goal is to follow those rules — industrial clarity, openness, users first, while giving people a new and confident experience.==


There's something to borrow from each store, but the guiding principle is this: don't take information away from the user — repurpose it into a design where they aren't inundated. That's consistent with Valve's own stated "users first" philosophy.


### Steam's Information Architecture Teardown

![IA Teardown](https://media.kaelub.com/steam/4.png)

The comparison left me with one tension to resolve — choice vs. clarity — so before drawing a single interface I needed to see where Steam's current flow actually loses people, and what a leaner path to the store's core function might look like. The information architecture is where the diagnosis from my research becomes more structured, apologies.

This stage set out to answer three questions:
```questions
Where does Steam's current user flow create friction, and why?
What is the minimum viable path to the store's core function: buying a game?
How do I cut clutter without stripping out the depth that makes Steam feel like Steam?
```

```button
View IA Teardown | https://www.figma.com/board/Ro4ndNMxhZkzaTmGVK9iri/Steam-store-map?node-id=0-1&t=C39SQUv861g9Ovbr-1 | FileMaps
```

I started by reframing what Steam is. The desk research established that it didn't begin as a shop, it grew out of distribution infrastructure, a system built to deliver and update software. So on the map I treated it that way: Steam is a directory and a distributor, and I leaned on a deliberately mechanical metaphor: river, veins, pipes, respawn point, to think about how a user moves through it. =="Monitors instead of gameboxes" is the modern state of the same idea: the storefront is plumbing dressed up as a shelf.==



### Mapping the current flow

The left side of the map is the Current Steam Store User Flow (Desktop Application) — and its density is the point. Boxed off on its own, it's a tangle of branching paths: layered submenus, multiple redundant routes to the same destination, and discovery surfaces (Discovery Queue, curators, recommendation rows) stacked on top of one another.

### Where this leaves the redesign

The architecture turns three sections of research into one decision the rest of the project can build on: keep Steam's distribution-era depth, but route it through a single, legible purchase spine, with discovery surfaced by the user's choice rather than forced on arrival. With the structure settled and the scope honestly bounded, ==the next step is to take this spine into wireframes — designing the home screen and game page that make this flow real.==
